# 🚀 Awesome Vibe Coding — Launch Plan

## North star

A successful launch = **300+ stars in week 1**, plus an HN front page appearance OR a viral X post (10k+ impressions).

That's the realistic ceiling for a solo-founder awesome list with a real audience behind it. Anything above that is gravy.

---

## Pre-launch (do this BEFORE you create the repo on GitHub)

### Day -7 to -3: Polish

1. **Final pass on the README.** Read it out loud. Cut anything that sounds like AI-speak. Replace generic praise ("powerful," "comprehensive") with specifics.
2. **Check every link.** Broken links on launch day kill credibility instantly. There are link-checker tools, or you can manually walk it.
3. **Add 2–3 of your own builds to "Real Builds."** It needs to feel populated, not empty. CEE and wunanddone are obvious. Maybe one more.
4. **Polish the contribution guidelines.** Set the bar high; quality over quantity attracts better contributors.
5. **Write your launch tweet thread** (template below). Don't write it on launch day — it'll be rushed.
6. **Write your HN submission text** (template below).
7. **Write your LinkedIn post** (template below).
8. **Write a Substack/Medium piece** going deeper on the *why* (template below).

### Day -3 to -1: Pre-warm

DM 8–12 people you actually know in the MWI / build-in-public / vibe-coder world. Template:

> "Hey [name] — heads up I'm launching a project Tuesday: an awesome list specifically for non-technical founders who vibe-code. It's the resource I wish I'd had. If you find it useful when it goes live, a star would mean a lot. I'll send the link Tuesday morning."

Don't ask for a quote. Don't ask for a retweet. Just let them know it's coming. People appreciate the heads-up; they don't appreciate being ambushed with asks.

### Day -1: Final checks

1. Push the repo public.
2. Add topics: `awesome`, `awesome-list`, `vibe-coding`, `ai`, `founder`, `nocode`, `claude-code`, `non-technical`.
3. Set a description: `A curated list of tools, prompts, and patterns for non-technical founders shipping real software with AI.`
4. Pin the repo on your profile.
5. Make sure your profile README is updated to mention it.
6. Make sure the social preview image is set (Settings → Social preview).

## Launch day — Tuesday or Wednesday, 9am ET

**Why Tuesday/Wednesday:** HN traffic peaks midweek. Monday people are catching up; Friday people are checking out. Avoid both.

**Why 9am ET:** Catches morning US, lunchtime EU. Bad for Asia but you can't optimize for everyone.

### 10:00am — Hacker News

This is the swing-for-the-fences moment. HN can give you 500 stars in 6 hours if it hits. It can also do nothing.

**Submission title format:**
`Show HN: Awesome Vibe Coding – tools and patterns for non-technical founders building with AI`

**Submission text (in the comment box that appears after submitting):**

```
I'm a non-technical founder who's been shipping software with AI for two years. CEE, wunanddone, several Embra products — all of them built with me as the operator and Claude Code / Cursor as the co-developer.

I made this list because every existing "AI coding tools" resource I found assumed I was already an engineer. None of them spoke to the actual constraints of building real products without a CS background.

This is the curated list of what's actually worked for me and the founders I trust.

The bar for inclusion: would this help a non-technical founder ship a real product? Not "is it trendy," not "is the team well-funded," just "does it work for the audience this list is for."

It's open source. If you've shipped real software as a non-technical founder, I'd love your contributions.
```

**HN tips:**

- Don't ask for upvotes. HN will downrank submissions that ask.
- Don't post and run. Stay around for the first 2 hours and reply to every comment thoughtfully.
- Don't get defensive. HN can be sharp. Roll with it.
- If you hit the front page (top 30), don't refresh constantly. Engage with comments.
- If you don't hit the front page, that's fine. Most submissions don't. The repo gets the link from your post anyway.

## Long-term: Compounding

Awesome lists compound differently than products. They get:

- **SEO traffic** for "awesome vibe coding," "AI coding tools for non-technical founders," etc.
- **Backlinks** from blog posts that reference the list
- **Recurring stars** as new builders discover it
- **Network effects** — every time someone you don't know PRs in their build or tool, your list grows in scope and credibility

To keep it alive:

1. **Review PRs weekly** — even one bad merge week kills momentum
2. **Quarterly clean-up** — remove dead links, kick out tools that died, add what's new
3. **Annual relaunch** — every 12 months, refresh the README with stats ("now with 500+ entries / 5000+ stars / 100+ contributors") and re-launch on X/HN. Lists can get a second wind this way.

## What success looks like

**Realistic:**
- 200–500 stars in week 1
- 5–10 community PRs in month 1
- 50–100 GitHub followers gained
- 1k–5k profile visits in week 1
- 1–2 inbound DMs from people who want to talk to you about Embra/MWI

**Stretch:**
- 1k+ stars in week 1 (HN front page or X-viral required)
- Featured in a tech newsletter (TLDR, Bytes, etc.)
- 200+ followers gained
- 10k+ profile visits

**Floor (still a win):**
- 50–100 stars
- 2–3 community PRs
- The repo exists, it's good, and it'll keep earning stars passively for years

Even the floor outcome is worth doing this. The artifact is durable.

## After this launches: what's next

Once awesome-vibe-coding has a few hundred stars and is earning passive traffic:

1. **Relaunch CEE** properly with the same playbook. Now you have an audience.
2. **Build one of the other repo ideas** — vibe-stack template or a Claude Code skills library. Now you have launch muscles and an audience.
3. **MWI essays drive traffic to the repo and vice versa.** Cross-link consistently.

The first launch teaches you the playbook. The second one is easier. The third one feels routine.
